Urban Futures: Partnering to Integrate Community Perspectives with Climate Modelling and Resilience

by the Urban Futures Organizing Team
Draft Final · June 23, 2026
In January 2026, the "Urban Futures: Co-Creating Climate Resilience in New York City" hackathon brought together data scientists, climate modellers, and New York City neighborhood leaders at the American Museum of Natural History. This three-day event explored how open data and climate modelling can address neighborhood-level impacts of climate change.

In January 2026, the "Urban Futures: Co-Creating Climate Resilience in New York City" hackathon brought together data scientists, climate modellers, and New York City neighborhood leaders at the American Museum of Natural History. This three-day hackathon was organized by a group of institutional and professional partners who were interested in bridging data science, climate modelling, and neighborhood-based climate resilience planning. The hackathon explored the uses of open data and climate modelling for addressing neighborhood-level impacts of climate change in New York City.

In designing a hackathon focused on neighborhood solutions, the organizing team recognized the importance of integrating and centering community perspectives and voices. Five groups of neighborhood leaders from five different New York City communities were invited to participate in the planning of "Urban Futures." These neighborhood leaders helped to frame the hackathon challenges and played leading roles in the hackathon itself. The incorporation of community perspectives in the collaborative setting of "Urban Futures" brought together different forms of expertise in ways that are not typical of a hackathon.

Whereas hackathon participants are often experts in computer science or data science, the neighborhood leaders who contributed to "Urban Futures" brought expertise in both resilience planning and the lived experiences of climate risk in their respective communities. Their expertise in local impacts and responses to climate change created a scaffold for planning and implementing the hackathon. Community participation meant technical resources needed to be accessible, the use of data needed to be grounded in local contexts, and participants needed time and space to understand different forms of expertise. This dynamic between different types of expertise provided the basis for innovative collaboration and solutions in "Urban Futures."

The vision to bring together diverse forms of expertise prompted the organizers to reconsider the conventional goals and frameworks of hackathons. The organizing team adapted many typical features of hackathons in order to center community perspectives. As with conventional hackathons, the team made datasets and collaborative platforms available to all participants. The team prepared new resources such as downscaled climate data and agentic AI tools for participants to explore and use. A group of mentors from Columbia University and NVIDIA were also available to support participants.

However, "Urban Futures" expanded upon the hackathon model by including neighborhood leaders in planning and facilitating the event. Each of the 15 participating teams was assigned one of five neighborhoods as a partner. The roles of the neighborhood leaders evolved throughout the hackathon, shifting from advisor at the start to team facilitators by the end. Further, a background in computer science or data science was not an explicit requirement for registration. Participants from fields such as architecture, urban planning, and environmental science also joined the hackathon. The variety of disciplines and forms of expertise resulted in proposals and outputs from participating teams that were grounded in local experiences. While neighborhood leaders gained exposure to new digital tools for assessing climate risk and developing solutions, hackathon participants had the opportunity to learn how their skills could apply to challenges facing real New York City neighborhoods.

Collaboration Phases

This report narrates how the organizing partners conceptualized and implemented "Urban Futures." By sharing lessons learned from "Urban Futures," the report offers guidance for institutions and organizations who are similarly interested in organizing community-centered hackathons. The organizing team included four partners: the American Museum of Natural History, who sponsored the hackathon through an Environmental Literacy grant awarded by the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA); the LEAP Science and Technology Center, a National Science Foundation-funded research center based at Columbia University focused on climate modelling, artificial intelligence, machine learning; the technology company NVIDIA, in particular its team working on diffusion models; and the design firm Starr Whitehouse Landscape Architects and Planners, who is involved in resilience planning in neighborhoods throughout New York City.

"Urban Futures" took place in three collaborative phases. First, there was a "planning" phase involving both organizing partners and neighborhood leaders. Second, there was a "creative" phase, which was the hackathon itself on January 15–17, 2026 at the Museum. Third, there was a post-hackathon "sharing" phase, during which teams presented their projects to stakeholders throughout New York City. During each of these phases, the organizing team made discoveries related to the challenges and potential innovations involved in combining different forms of expertise in a collaborative setting like a hackathon. Each of these discoveries emerged from the foundational goal of "Urban Futures," which was to meaningfully integrate community perspectives with the hackathon.

"My biggest takeaway was that it's so important to have the right problem identified. Talking with the community partners was crucial to understand exactly what the neighborhood needed, and not just creating something we think they needed or that was convenient to make with the given datasets."
Phase 1

Planning

The organizing partners worked together for eight months to plan "Urban Futures." The planning process involved conceptualizing the event, preparing data resources, recruiting participants, and identifying neighborhood leaders. From the beginning of the planning process, an explicit goal was to center community perspectives in the hackathon. The first discovery the team made together was that this decision would necessitate that the team develop a shared understanding of the role of neighborhood leaders in climate resilience – both in New York City generally and in the hackathon. With the exception of the design partner, the organizing partners lacked direct experience engaging and collaborating with neighborhood-based organizations involved in resilience planning in New York City. The organizing partners therefore had to better understand who the neighborhood leaders were in order to plan the data resources and collaborative framework for the hackathon.

Depending on how they are mapped, New York City has between 250 and 300 neighborhoods. The city's governance structure also includes 59 Community Districts, which are represented by volunteer Community Boards. Within these communities, neighborhood-level organizations serve a variety of purposes: open space and waterfront access advocacy, public realm management, housing development, health and social services, and educational, youth, and cultural programming. Over the last 15 years, New York City's government has increasingly focused on planning for climate risks such as flooding and heat. Neighborhood-level organizations have had a role in this planning as intermediary representatives between city government and the broader community.

The organizing partners invited neighborhood leaders from five communities – one from each New York City borough – to participate in the hackathon. The neighborhoods included Soundview in the Bronx, Flushing in Queens, East Harlem in Manhattan, Brownsville in Brooklyn, and Stapleton in Staten Island. All of these neighborhoods have been involved in "Climate Strong Communities." Through this program, the New York City Mayor's Office of Climate and Environmental Justice works with community organizations to envision a resilient built environment. In addition to their individual roles in their organizations, each of the neighborhood leaders brought experience working with urban data to plan for resilience with city government.

One key planning decision for "Urban Futures" was who would define the challenges given to participating teams. The organizing partners opted to create an open framework for the hackathon. The team worked with the neighborhood leaders in the months before the hackathon to develop a series of "prompts" based on the lived experience of climate risk in each neighborhood and programs run by the organizations represented by the neighborhood leaders. The "prompts" would be presented as starting points in the hackathon, but not inflexibly defined problems teams would be required to solve. These prompts provided one dimension of the "scaffold," that of community knowledge.

The organizing partners soon discovered that the decision to create an open framework through community-envisioned "prompts" had implications for other planning considerations. NVIDIA and LEAP, for example, worked together to equip teams to take on the prompts through downscaled climate modelling data and agentic AI tools. The resolution of downscaled data, however, is different from the open data provided by New York City to plan for climate resilience that neighborhood leaders typically use. The team had to discuss and understand potential use cases for downscaled data versus open data and how data scientists might engage these resources versus neighborhood leaders. It was also important to understand the breadth of existing data resources available through New York City city agencies and the New York City Panel on Climate Change – and that the neighborhood leaders' organizations actively use these resources.

The decision to center community perspectives also informed how applicants for "Urban Futures" were reviewed and how teams were curated. The organizing partners received more than 150 applications for 75 seats. The application was open to students and professionals from throughout the New York City region. While the organizers received applications from computer scientists and data scientists, as expected, applications were also submitted by architects, urban planners, educators, and environmental scientists. The organizing partners admitted many applicants who did not have computer coding skills, but brought knowledge of urban planning and sustainability. These non-computer scientists were mixed in with the other applicants so each of the 15 teams had a balance of skills including coding, visualization, planning, and human centered design. Further, LEAP and NVIDIA recruited mentors from both academic and private settings to support the interdisciplinary teams, assuring that each team would have capacity to manipulate the data resources.

At the same time that planning decisions were guided by the prioritization of community perspectives, each organizing partner retained its own goals related to organizational or institutional mission. The Museum, for example, was interested to explore how public programming related to climate could extend to new audiences within the city. LEAP and NVIDIA desired to gauge accessibility to and utility of downscaled climate data and climate-related agentic AI tools for broader and more diversely-skilled audiences. One of LEAP's organization goals, in particular, is knowledge transfer, and so LEAP was eager to engage a broad audience with climate modelling. Ultimately, the team found that these goals complemented the bigger picture objective of centering community perspectives.

Phase 2

Creating

The hackathon was scheduled over three days. On the first day, the participants of the 15 teams met each other and the neighborhood leaders. Each leader was assigned to three teams such that there were three teams representing each of the five neighborhoods. The teams spent the first day hearing from the neighborhood leaders and refining a project concept based on the prompts. The second day was a day for collaboration and production with both neighborhood leaders and mentors circulating to support the teams. On the third day, the teams polished their projects and made presentations during a public program at the Museum.

The neighborhood leaders played a central role in kicking off "Urban Futures" on the hackathon's first day. Each neighborhood leader gave a presentation that narrated their community's history and the climate risks faced by residents. Their presentations also included problem statements or "prompts" for the teams to consider. These presentations – which highlighted the lived experience of climate risk and the unique physical characteristics of climate vulnerability in each neighborhood – created a conceptual scaffold for the teams to work within. Following the presentations, the teams met with the neighborhood leaders to further understand the communities and envision their projects.

Two dynamics that the organizers did not expect evolved over the three-day hackathon, leading to further discoveries. These discoveries regarded the scope and scale of team projects. First, most teams spent the entirety of the first day refining their project concept and consulting with the neighborhood leaders. The organizers observed team members interviewing neighborhood leaders at length, trying to understand the nuances of the neighborhood leaders' prompts and the physical conditions of the neighborhoods themselves. The degree of fidelity to the neighborhood leaders' prompts was remarkable in the sense that within the hackathon framework, the prompts were starting points and the teams were, in reality, free to create whatever they wanted with the data resources. Further, once teams had narrowed down project concepts, they started to check in with each other in order to differentiate their work. The neighborhood leaders played a role in this, steering individual teams in different directions so that project concepts were not redundant. During the second day of the hackathon, the mentors also differentiated projects from a technical standpoint, recommending workflows and methodologies so that each team was creating something unique from a computer science perspective.

Another discovery concerned the geographic scope of teams' project concepts. Every team scoped their project within the boundaries of the neighborhood leader's community. This was also unexpected because the teams were explicitly told that it was not a requirement to work at the neighborhood scale, especially since the resolution of many of the available data resources were at the city scale. The fidelity to the neighborhood scale reflected a kind of commitment or client relationship that developed between the teams and the neighborhood leaders. The teams were genuinely committed to the challenges of the neighborhoods and motivated by the opportunity to present solutions. As facilitators in this process on the hackathon's second day, the neighborhood leaders added an experiential dimension to the scaffold.

The significance of the conceptual scaffold combining lived experience and a physical knowledge of each neighborhood was underscored by the projects the teams developed. The projects presented on the hackathon's final day reflected how the teams interpreted the neighborhood leader prompts using available data and a variety of data science methodologies. All of the projects addressed a specific climate risk and its relationship to one or more urban systems or features of the built environment. Overall, the 15 team projects fell into one of four categories of solutions:

  • Asset Mapping Tools: Maps and data dashboards providing information on access to social infrastructure or climate risk reduction measures like green infrastructure.
  • Storytelling / Data Visualization: Projects narrating climate risk and impacts on the built environment through visualization of statistical climate data or mapping.
  • Routing Tools: Projects using routing algorithms to identify optimal emergency evacuation pathways or locations for cooling centers; sometimes coupled with reports generated by AI agents.
  • Scenario Planning Tools: Projects visualizing resilience measures such as building weatherization and increased urban tree canopy, evaluating the co-benefits of these measures.

Blue Whales · East Harlem
Mapping the Drainage Breakdown Loop in East Harlem
The goal of this project was to leverage NYC Open Data to highlight a community-identified environmental justice issue in East Harlem. Community reporting indicates that public waste bins are sparse or have been removed, residential trash pickup is inconsistent, and street litter is a persistent issue...

The goal of this project was to leverage NYC Open Data to highlight a community-identified environmental justice issue in East Harlem. Community reporting (interviews with the project's community partners, 311 Data, DSNY Litter Basket Data) indicates that public waste bins are sparse or have been removed, residential trash pickup is inconsistent, and street litter is a persistent issue. To estimate how likely litter is to enter storm drains, we measured the distance between catch basins and public trash bins. In East Harlem, the average distance was 46.7 meters, compared to 19.8 meters in the neighboring Upper East Side. We defined high clogging risk as catch basins with no trash bin within a 50-meter radius. Overlaying these high-risk basins with current and projected stormwater flood zones demonstrates that these areas also have conditions that make drainage failure due to clogging more likely. Compounding flood vulnerability in East Harlem demonstrates how inequitable city sanitation services are a climate risk.

Megadolons · Brownsville
OUR-ERA: Emergency Routing Assistant
OUR-ERA is a climate-aware pedestrian routing assistant built for Brownsville, Brooklyn, NYC's highest heat-vulnerability neighborhood and a walking-dependent, majority-NYCHA community...

OUR-ERA is a climate-aware pedestrian routing assistant built for Brownsville, Brooklyn, NYC's highest heat-vulnerability neighborhood and a walking-dependent, majority-NYCHA community. The system accepts natural-language queries and returns optimized walking routes to emergency services, healthcare, cooling centers, and community resources using a real street network with climate risk embedded in every edge. Flood risk (FEMA stormwater maps), heat vulnerability index, air quality, shade, and grade penalties are combined into a per-segment weight, so the routing engine favors safe paths over short ones when conditions are dangerous. A local large language model translates plain language (including non-English queries) into structured routing calls, keeping all computation offline and private. The network covers 7,600 nodes and surfaces over 1,200 points of interest across Brownsville, with four route variants: shortest, flattest, balanced, and climate-safest.

Phase 3

Sharing

The winning projects highlighted here demonstrate how the participating teams combined multiple data resources and grounded their projects in what they learned from the neighborhood leaders about the lived experience of climate risk in New York City neighborhoods. Because of the scaffold provided by the neighborhood leaders, the projects were nuanced and multilayered, addressing the interconnections between climate risk and the built environment.

The organizing partners are still discovering how the ideas generated during "Urban Futures" have taken on a life after the event. The utility of the real world application of climate modelling and data science that was the intent of the hackathon was underscored by how community partners followed up with the teams after "Urban Futures." Community partners invited teams to present their projects to Community Boards in Brownsville, East Harlem, and Stapleton. The New York City Mayor's Office of Climate and Environmental Justice helped to facilitate meetings between teams and the New York City Housing Authority and Department of Sanitation. Some teams have also connected with elected officials to share their projects. For the team members, these experiences raise awareness of career pathways and settings where computer and data science skills can be helpful to communities.

"A takeaway I've gained from participation is an appreciation for the community organizations and communities who are dealing with climate problems. We hear it in the news but through attempting to form solutions, we gained a much deeper insight into what's actually happening."

Conclusions

The decision to make centering community perspectives the guiding planning principle for "Urban Futures" was the main distinguishing factor making this hackathon different from others. Doing so introduced the challenge of integrating different forms of expertise and knowledge to the forefront of planning the event. The organizing partners found that neighborhood expertise could provide a scaffold to support participant experiences in which different world views and skillsets could be interwoven.

Further, through collaboration with neighborhood leaders, students, and professionals from different disciplines (and subsequent follow up), participants were able to immediately see the practical application of their work. This was especially meaningful for computer and data science students, many of whose work with climate modelling has primarily been research focused. These participants had the opportunity to help local organizations and see their own skills and knowledge areas expand in the context of other disciplines. Overall, these experiences point to avenues for collaboration for institutions and other private sector organizations who are interested in advancing solutions for resilience through open data and climate modelling.

"I really enjoyed the opportunity to interact and collaborate on a shared cause with driven people from a diverse variety of highly relevant backgrounds and expertise. The most personally rewarding part of the experience for me was making progress on understanding how my skills might fit into a more solutions oriented team as opposed to a purely research team."
Note on New York City Data

It is important to recognize that there is a breadth of open data available for New York City related to climate change. This includes projections for sea level rise and coastal storms created by the New York City Panel on Climate Change, which is a legally-mandated independent and scientific advisory body. Many city agencies and government offices have also created datasets related to climate, such as the New York City Department of Environmental Protection's modelling of cloudburst projections and the City of New York City Council Data Science team's urban heat model. These resources are used in planning for resilience across the city. The organizing partners recognized that the accessibility of these data resources made planning events like "Urban Futures" easier than if fewer open data resources were available. Many other cities such as Miami, San Francisco, and Seattle have substantial open data resources available, but this isn't the case in all communities.
